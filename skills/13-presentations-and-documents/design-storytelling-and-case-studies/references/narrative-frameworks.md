# Reference: Narrative Frameworks — the named structures and how to choose

*Source: Lyle H. Sandler, *Universal Principles of Storytelling for Designers: 100 Key Concepts*
(2023) — extracted as a catalogue of named narrative structures. This reference supplies the
**structure** for telling a story; it does not supply taste, craft, or the artifact. Used naively,
a narrative structure will happily wrap a templated product — see the fence below.*

> **The fence (restated).** Every structure here organises the *telling* of **real, authored work**.
> It never substitutes for the working demo and never decorates slop
> (`doctrine/references/creative-selection-and-taste.md`). The structures are conflict-and-shape
> templates; the **picture inside the frame must be a real thing you built and a real outcome you
> can prove.** Pick a structure to make true work legible — never to make thin work sound thick.

---

## Why structure at all

A pile of facts is not a story, and an audience does not *remember* a pile of facts. A narrative
structure does three jobs: it creates **productive tension** (so the audience leans in), it
**sequences evidence** so each beat lands at the right moment, and it makes the **transformation
felt**, not merely stated. The structure is how a true account becomes a *memorable* one.

The two load-bearing principles that every structure below depends on:

- **Contrast, beginning-to-end.** A distinct *otherness* must exist between the start and the end
  of the story. The **before/after delta IS the narrative.** If you open on the solution (the
  water-filtration system) instead of the problem (the drought), the audience never feels the size
  of the transformation, and the work reads as inevitable rather than authored. *Always establish
  the "before" with real stakes before you reveal the "after."* This is the single most important
  rule for case studies and pitch decks.
- **Conflict / the trigger (the wicked problem).** Story needs a named problem with stakes — the
  *why behind the what*. In design, the conflict is the user's real, often messy ("wicked") problem.
  Name it, give it stakes, and let it create the tension the rest of the structure resolves. (Note:
  one structure below — Kishōtenketsu — deliberately works *without* conflict.)

## The structures

### 1. Three-Act
**Shape:** Setup → Confrontation → Resolution.
- **Act I (Setup):** the protagonist (the user) and their world; the problem appears.
- **Act II (Confrontation):** the struggle — constraints, failed approaches, the rising difficulty.
- **Act III (Resolution):** the authored decision lands; the new state; proof.

**Use for:** the default, clean pitch or case study. The most robust, lowest-risk structure when
you are unsure — it maps cleanly onto problem → work → outcome. Hard to get wrong.

### 2. Freytag's Pyramid
**Shape:** Exposition → Rising Action → **Climax** → Falling Action → Denouement (catharsis).
A more dramatic, five-beat refinement of Three-Act, with an explicit single **climax**.
- Exposition: the user and the stakes.
- Rising action: the escalating difficulty / the constraints tightening.
- **Climax: the authored decision** — the one defensible, taste-driven choice (the turning point).
- Falling action: how the decision played out, what shipped.
- Denouement: the settled new normal; the measured outcome; the lesson.

**Use for:** a richer, longer case study where there is a genuine single turning-point decision
worth dramatising and you have the room to build to it. Overkill for a 3-slide pitch.

### 3. Pixar ("Story Spine")
**Shape:** *Once upon a time… Every day… Until one day… Because of that… Because of that…
Until finally…* (and the resolved new "every day").
- "Every day" establishes the painful status quo (the before, with stakes).
- "Until one day" is the trigger / the insight / the authored intervention.
- "Because of that…" chains the consequences to the outcome.

**Use for:** a **relatable, human change** story — onboarding, a workflow that was painful and got
better, a service redesign. Excellent when the audience must *feel* the user's day-to-day. Warm and
accessible; the structure itself foregrounds the user-as-protagonist.

### 4. Kishōtenketsu (the no-villain, four-act structure)
**Shape:** **Ki** (introduction) → **Shō** (development) → **Ten** (twist / unexpected turn) →
**Ketsu** (reconciliation). Crucially, it has **no conflict and no antagonist** — the "Ten" is an
unexpected *shift in perspective*, not a clash.

**Use for:** **explanatory / product narratives that don't want a villain** — explaining how a
system works, a thoughtful product story, a concept walkthrough, or any case where forcing a
manufactured "enemy" would feel cheap or untrue. When there was no dramatic struggle, do not invent
one; use Kishōtenketsu and let the "twist" be the non-obvious insight or the elegant reframe.

### 5. Contrast, Beginning-to-End (the before/after spine)
**Shape:** open hard on the **before** (problem + stakes) → reveal the **after** → let the *delta*
carry the meaning. Not a full plot so much as the **organising principle** every case study obeys
(see "Why structure" above). Often used *inside* Three-Act or Pixar to guarantee the transformation
is felt.

**Use for:** any case study, always — it is the spine, not an alternative. Reach for it explicitly
when the result is genuinely strong but the audience is at risk of seeing it as obvious/inevitable.

### 6. Peak-End Rule (a shaping rule, not a full arc)
**Principle:** people judge and remember an experience by its **most intense moment (the peak)** and
its **end** — not by the average. So **design the peak and design the close deliberately.**
- Place your **strongest single piece of evidence** (the shocking number, the dramatic before/after,
  the live demo moment) at the **peak**.
- End on the transformed "after" + **one** CTA — never trail off on a feature list or a thank-you.

**Use for:** layering over *any* of the structures above to decide *where the strongest evidence
goes* and *how the telling closes*. Always apply it. (Pairs with the deck-system S.T.A.R. moment,
which is the *delivery* expression of the same idea.)

### Supporting devices
- **Rule of Three.** Three is the smallest number that makes a pattern; group reasons, options, or
  proof points in threes for memorability — and cut the fourth.
- **Show, don't tell.** Replace adjectives ("intuitive," "delightful") with the evidence that earns
  them (the demo, the metric, the user quote). Every claim shows its proof or is cut.
- **Kill Your Darlings.** Cut any beat, slide, or detail that does not serve the through-line —
  even a good one. The writing-side twin of the doctrine's "edit for less."

## Decision table — choosing the structure

| If the story is… | Use | Because |
|---|---|---|
| A clean problem → work → outcome, and you're unsure | **Three-Act** | Lowest-risk default; maps directly onto a design case study. |
| Rich, with one genuine turning-point decision to dramatise | **Freytag** | Gives an explicit climax to land the authored decision. |
| A relatable human change (onboarding, painful→better workflow) | **Pixar** | "Every day / until one day" makes the user's day felt; user-as-protagonist baked in. |
| Explanatory, with **no real villain or struggle** | **Kishōtenketsu** | No-conflict structure; the "twist" is an insight, so you never fake an enemy. |
| Strong result the audience might see as obvious | **Contrast** (as the spine) | Forces the before/after delta to be felt, not assumed. |
| *Any of the above* | **+ Peak-End** | Decide where the strongest evidence sits and how the telling closes. |

**Choosing rule of thumb:** start from the *truth of the work*. Was there a dramatic single
decision? → Freytag. A daily human pain that eased? → Pixar. No conflict at all? → Kishōtenketsu.
Not sure? → Three-Act. Then **always** add Contrast (the spine) and Peak-End (the shaping). Never
choose a structure that requires inventing stakes or a struggle that did not happen — that is the
fence breaking.

## The fence, one more time

If applying a structure tempts you to *manufacture* a conflict, a journey, or an outcome the
evidence does not support — **stop.** The structure has exceeded the work. Either find the real
evidence (and tell the true, smaller story well) or do not tell it yet. A beautifully structured
story about thin or unbuilt work is the exact failure mode the Mission exists to defeat: it makes
the convergent mean *look* authored. The structure is the frame; the authored work is the picture
(`doctrine/references/creative-selection-and-taste.md`; `doctrine/design-doctrine.md` §0, §2).
